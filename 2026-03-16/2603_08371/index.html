Overview

This paper addresses the problem of misaligned incentives in modern machine learning leaderboards, where competing model developers engage in "benchmaxxing" (strategic post-training optimized for benchmark task distributions rather than general capability), leading to unstable, misleading rankings that do not reflect true latent model quality. The key insight is to formalize benchmarking as a Stackelberg game between a leaderboard designer and competing developers: the paper proves that standard evaluation protocols (without additional guardrails) have no pure-strategy Nash equilibrium when the cost of just-overtake effort is less than the reward gap for at least one rank, explaining persistent arms-race dynamics and opaque strategic behavior. However, under mild empirically validated conditions, the recently proposed tune-before-test (TbT) protocol induces a unique, stable pure-strategy Nash equilibrium where developers invest no additional benchmark-specific effort, and leaderboard rankings exactly match the ordering of models by latent general capability.

Key Contributions

  • Formalize leaderboard evaluation as a Stackelberg game for the first time, capturing the strategic interaction between a designer choosing evaluation protocols and developers optimizing post-training effort to maximize rank-based rewards.
  • Prove that standard benchmark protocols (with no TbT intervention) admit no pure-strategy Nash equilibrium when the cost of just-overtake effort is less than the reward gap for any rank, providing a theoretical explanation for the unstable, arms-race dynamics observed in real-world LLM leaderboards.
  • Prove that under mild, empirically verified structural assumptions on post-training performance scaling and effort costs, the TbT protocol induces a unique pure-strategy Nash equilibrium where all developers choose zero additional benchmark-specific effort, and resulting rankings exactly reflect latent model capability ordering.
  • Show TbT has a stabilizing effect: increasing the baseline TbT effort uniformly applied to all models raises the minimal effort required to overtake higher-ranked competitors in practice, with only small TbT levels required to eliminate strategic overtaking incentives in practice.
  • Empirically validate all core theoretical assumptions on real post-training trajectories of Qwen2.5 models across 9 benchmarks, demonstrating that just 3,000 TbT steps raise the minimal effort required to change any rank to over 384,000 additional post-training steps.

Method

"We model benchmarking as a Stackelberg game between a benchmark designer who chooses an evaluation protocol and multiple model developers who compete simultaneously in a subgame given by the designer's choice."

The game proceeds in two stages: first, the leaderboard designer commits to a TbT baseline effort level $\etbt$ (the amount of benchmark-specific fine-tuning applied uniformly to all submitted models prior to evaluation). Second, after observing $\etbt$, model developers simultaneously choose additional benchmark-specific effort $e_i \ge 0$ to maximize their utility. Setting $\etbt = 0$ recovers the standard leaderboard protocol with no TbT intervention. The leaderboard designer’s objective is to maximize the alignment between leaderboard rankings and true latent model capability, with the overall solution concept being the Stackelberg-Nash equilibrium, where the designer commits to a protocol in anticipation of the developer subgame equilibrium response.

"Each competitor has a model of a latent quality --- unknown to the benchmark designer --- and can inflate the observed model score by allocating additional resources to benchmark-specific improvements at a cost."

Latent general model capability is represented by a scalar parameter $\theta_i \in \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}$, with higher values indicating stronger general performance, and models are indexed in decreasing order of capability ($\theta_1 > \theta_2 > \dots > \theta_n$). Benchmark performance is captured by the post-effort score function $v(\theta,e): \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0} \times \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0} \to [0,1]$, where $e$ is total benchmark-specific effort. Model developer utility is defined as:

$$U_i(e_i; \textbf{e}_{-i}, \etbt) = R_{\operatorname{rank}(v_i)} - c(e_i)$$ where $v_i = v(\theta_i, \etbt + e_i)$, $\operatorname{rank}(v_i) = 1 + \sum_{j \in N \setminus \{i\}} \mathbb{I}\{v_j > v_i\}$, $R_j$ is the non-increasing rank-based reward (higher rank yields larger reward), and $c(e)$ is a convex, non-decreasing effort cost function satisfying $c(0) = 0$ and $\lim_{e \to \infty} c(e) = \infty$. The paper imposes three mild structural assumptions on the post-effort score function, all empirically validated: 1. Monotonicity in capability: For fixed effort, higher capability yields higher scores: $\partial_\theta v(\theta,e) > 0$. 2. Diminishing returns in effort: Effort improves performance with decreasing marginal returns, and scores converge to a model-specific saturation limit: $\partial_e v(\theta,e) \ge 0$, $\partial_{ee} v(\theta,e) \le 0$, and $v^\infty(\theta) = \lim_{e \to \infty} v(\theta,e)$ exists and is finite. 3. Non-decreasing effort gaps: The additional effort required for a lower-capability model to match the score of a higher-capability model weakly increases as target score increases. An empirically validated functional form for the post-effort score function following generalized power-law scaling is: $$\sigma^{-1}\left(\tilde{v}(\theta,e)\right) = \alpha(\theta) + \beta(\theta)\log(1+e)$$ where $\tilde{v}(\theta,e)$ is the normalized score between model-specific lower $L(\theta)$ and upper $U(\theta)$ performance bounds, $\sigma^{-1}$ is the logit link function, $\alpha(\theta)$ is the baseline logit performance at zero effort, and $\beta(\theta)$ is the effort scaling coefficient (higher values indicate more efficient use of additional effort). > "Under mild conditions, a recently proposed evaluation protocol, called tune-before-test, induces a benchmark with a unique Nash equilibrium that ranks models by latent quality." The core theoretical results are built around the just-overtake effort $e_r^\req(\etbt)$, defined as the minimal additional effort required for the $r$-th capability-ranked model to overtake the model one rank above it at the baseline TbT score: $$e_r^\req(\etbt) = \inf\left\{e \ge 0: v(\theta_r, \etbt + e) > s_{r-1}(\etbt)\right\}$$ where $s_{r}(\etbt) = v(\theta_r, \etbt)$ is the baseline TbT score of model $r$. The paper proves that the all-zero additional effort profile $\textbf{e} = (0, \dots, 0)$ is a pure-strategy Nash equilibrium (PNE) if and only if for all $r \in \{2, \dots, n\}$: $$c\left(e_r^\req(\etbt)\right) \ge R_{r-1} - R_r$$ If this condition is violated for any $r$, no PNE exists, leading to persistent overtaking incentives and arms-race dynamics. An intermediate key result (Proposition 1) confirms that if a pure-strategy Nash equilibrium exists, the resulting leaderboard ranking strictly preserves the latent capability ordering of submitted models. The paper demonstrates that higher TbT levels raise $e_r^\req(\etbt)$, making overtaking more costly. The stabilizing TbT threshold is the minimal $\etbt$ satisfying the equilibrium condition for all $r$: $$\etbt^\star = \inf\left\{\etbt \ge 0: c\left(e_r^\req(\etbt)\right) \ge R_{r-1} - R_r, \forall r \in \{2, \dots, n\}\right\}$$

Any $\etbt \ge \etbt^\star$ induces a unique PNE at zero additional effort, with rankings exactly matching latent capability ordering.

Experiments

The authors validate their theoretical assumptions and results via controlled post-training experiments on Qwen2.5 models of varying sizes (used as a proxy for latent capability $\theta$) across 9 benchmarks including Winogrande. They fit the generalized power-law scaling function to observed post-training trajectories, confirming all three structural assumptions on the post-effort score function hold in practice.

Key experimental results show that increasing TbT level rapidly raises the cost of overtaking adjacent ranks:

  • At $\etbt = 0$ (standard protocol), only a small number of additional post-training steps are required to change at least one model's rank.
  • At $\etbt = 3,000$ steps, the minimal effort required to change any rank rises to 384,668 additional steps, making strategic overtaking prohibitively costly for nearly all practical reward schemes.

Limitations & Future Work

Limitations

  1. The theoretical framework uses simplifying assumptions that abstract from real-world leaderboard features including noisy evaluation scores, uncertainty about competitor latent capabilities, heterogeneous non-separable effort cost structures, and dynamic repeated submission dynamics.
  2. The TbT intervention is not costless: it requires additional evaluation compute and latency, and may conflate a model's underlying general capability with its ability to adapt to fine-tuning during the TbT step.
  3. The current analysis focuses on pure-strategy Nash equilibria, and does not characterize mixed-strategy equilibria that may exist when the pure-strategy equilibrium condition is violated.

Future Work

  1. Extend the framework to account for noisy evaluations, uncertain competitor capabilities, and flexible heterogeneous cost structures to improve real-world robustness.
  2. Quantify the trade-offs between TbT intervention cost, ranking stability, and alignment of leaderboard rankings with real-world out-of-distribution generalization performance.
  3. Explore additional evaluation protocol design levers beyond TbT to further align developer incentives with general capability improvements rather than benchmark-specific optimization.
  4. Study the dynamics of repeated leaderboard submissions and how TbT interacts with submission limits and other common leaderboard rules.
